Module 01
Technical Strategy & Vision
Shape engineering direction beyond your team
Most engineers wait to be given a strategy. Staff engineers create one. This module teaches you to look further ahead than your sprint, identify where your systems need to go, and write the documents that get others moving in that direction — even without a title that says "Architect." By the end, you'll have a real technical vision and a real RFC in circulation, not as homework, but as actual work that matters to your organization.
Weeks 1–2
Learning Objectives
Write a compelling 1-page technical vision that engineers quote and leaders rally behind
Author RFCs that move decisions from Slack debate to signed-off alignment
Structure build vs. buy decisions with enough rigor to survive VP scrutiny
Facilitate architecture reviews as a thought partner, not just a gatekeeper
Build and present a tech radar that gives your org a shared vocabulary for risk
Topics Covered
What a technical vision is — and why most are vague, ignored, and useless
The 6–18 month planning horizon: far enough to matter, near enough to act on
RFC anatomy: problem, proposal, alternatives, trade-offs, decision record
IETF RFC 2119 precision language: when to use MUST vs. SHOULD vs. MAY
Tech radar: adopt / trial / assess / hold — and how to run a team session
Architecture Decision Records (ADRs) for teams that won't write docs
🎯Management Tips — Strategy
Anchor early, adjust later
Publish a rough draft of your vision before it's polished. Imperfect early drafts create alignment anchors — people react to something concrete rather than arguing from scratch. Your v0.1 shapes the eventual v1.0 far more than any later revision. Waiting until it's "ready" means someone else sets the anchor.
Pre-brief key skeptics
Before presenting any RFC or proposal in a group meeting, have 1:1 conversations with your top 2–3 skeptics. People who've been pre-briefed become allies in the meeting rather than blockers. The group meeting should be ratification, not discovery. Surprises in group settings create defensiveness that's hard to undo.
Make inaction a visible choice
When proposing change, explicitly name "do nothing" as Option C — with its costs and risks spelled out in the same level of detail as your alternatives. This reframes inertia as a decision with consequences, not a safe default. Leaders who see the cost of inaction are far more likely to move.
Step-by-Step Exercises
E1
Write your technical vision (1 page)
Pick one system or domain you own. Write a crisp 1-page vision document: where the system is today, why it falls short, and what a better future looks like in 12–18 months. The page constraint is the exercise — if you can't explain it in one page, the vision isn't clear yet. Clarity of thought precedes clarity of document.
1Write a 2-sentence "current state" that a non-engineer could understand
2List 3 specific problems the current state causes — with real examples, not hypotheticals
3Write a "north star" paragraph: what does great look like in 18 months?
4Add 3–4 guiding principles that will shape decisions along the way
5Share with two peers and your manager before it feels "ready" — use the anchor tactic
~3 hrsWeek 1Written artifact
E2
Write a real RFC on a pending decision
Identify a real architectural decision your team has been informally debating or avoiding. Write a complete RFC — not a thought experiment, a real one. Pre-brief your top skeptic, then send async to at least 3 stakeholders with a specific "feedback by [date]" ask — not "let me know what you think." Track every objection and how you resolved it. This is the single highest-leverage exercise in the curriculum.
1Choose a real open decision — something your team has been avoiding
2Write: Background, Problem Statement, Proposed Solution, Alternatives Considered, Trade-offs, Open Questions
3Pre-brief your top skeptic 1:1 before the async send
4Send with a hard feedback deadline and a clear decision date
5Incorporate feedback, update the doc, and close with a written Decision Record
~4 hrsWeek 1–2Written artifact + feedback loop
E3
Build a tech radar for your stack
List every significant technology, framework, tool, and platform your team uses or is considering. Place each in one of four buckets with a one-sentence rationale. Facilitate a 30-min team session to review, debate, and adjust. Present the final radar to your EM or director as your team's shared technical stance — not as homework, but as a real deliverable.
1List every technology in use or actively being evaluated
2Classify: Adopt (proven, use now), Trial (promising, experiment), Assess (watch closely), Hold (phase out)
3Write a one-sentence rationale for each classification
4Facilitate a 30-min team session — expect debate on 2–3 items; that debate is the value
5Present to leadership as a shared technical position, not a personal opinion
~2 hrs solo + 30 min teamWeek 2Facilitation

Module 02
Influence, Alignment & Organizational Navigation
Win hearts, rooms, and decisions — without a reporting line
Org dynamics isn't dirty — it's the operating system of every company. Staff engineers who ignore it get good work quietly buried. Those who master it create outsized impact on decisions they don't control. This module teaches you to map real power (not org charts), build alliances before you need them, navigate competing agendas without picking sides, and land decisions in environments where no one owes you a "yes."
Weeks 3–4
Learning Objectives
Map the real power structure — not the org chart, the actual influence network
Build strategic alliances before you need them, not during a crisis
Make async written persuasion your most reliable tool for winning alignment
Navigate competing team agendas without losing credibility or integrity
Handle visible disagreement with senior leaders gracefully and effectively
Understand executive decision styles and adapt your pitch accordingly
Topics Covered
Stakeholder mapping: formal authority vs. informal influence vs. veto power
The "relationship bank account" model: building trust before you need it
The pre-brief strategy: converting opponents into allies before the meeting
Navigating competing priorities without alienating either team
Reading executive decision styles: data-driven, intuition-led, consensus-seeking
Disagreeing with senior leaders without burning bridges or losing credibility
🎭Management Tips — Influence & Navigation
The 3-meeting rule
Never try to land a major decision cold in a group meeting. First: meet 1:1 with each key stakeholder to understand concerns. Second: send a written alignment summary ("here's where I think we are"). Third: the group meeting is final confirmation only. Surprises in group settings create defensiveness that can stall decisions for weeks.
Deposit before you withdraw
Build relationship capital with stakeholders before you need anything from them. Help with their priorities. Amplify their work. Give credit publicly. When you later need their support for your initiative, they're already in your corner — not evaluating whether to trust you. Relationship debt is as real as technical debt.
Name the tension, own the neutrality
When two teams have competing priorities, don't pick a side — name the tension explicitly: "I see Team A needs X while Team B needs Y, and those are in direct conflict." Then facilitate the trade-off conversation. The person who names the problem clearly usually gets to shape the solution.
Strategic visibility without self-promotion
Work that no one sees doesn't advance your mission or your career. Send brief async updates on significant work — not to self-promote, but to keep stakeholders informed. Monthly "what I've been working on" notes to your manager and skip-level build reputation over time without ever feeling like a sales pitch.
Disagree and commit — publicly
When a decision goes against your recommendation, say explicitly: "I disagree with this direction, and I'm fully committed to making it work." This is more powerful than silent compliance. It preserves your credibility, shows leadership maturity, and creates a documented moment you can revisit fairly if the decision doesn't pan out.
Hear the worry beneath the question
Executives rarely ask exactly what they mean. "Is this scalable?" often means "Will this embarrass us in 18 months?" "What's the timeline?" often means "Will this slip like the last project?" Learn to address the underlying worry — not just the surface question. This is what makes senior leaders trust your judgment.
Step-by-Step Exercises
E4
Map the real influence network (not the org chart)
Org charts show reporting lines. Power maps show how decisions actually get made. For your current initiative, map every person who has formal authority, informal influence, or practical veto power. Identify whose support you still need, whose objections you haven't addressed, and who can open doors you can't reach directly. Then make a concrete 2-week plan to close those gaps.
1List all stakeholders: direct teammates, adjacent teams, executives, and silent influencers
2For each: their primary concern, influence level (high/med/low), and current stance (champion/neutral/skeptic)
3Draw influence lines between people — who shapes whose thinking?
4Identify your relationship gaps: who do you have low capital with and why?
5Write a specific 2-week plan to make one deposit with each gap stakeholder
~2.5 hrsWeek 3Written artifact
E5
Rewrite a decision doc to pre-answer objections
Take a decision doc or proposal you've already written. List the top 3 objections stakeholders raised or would likely raise. Rewrite the doc to incorporate and defuse each objection before anyone has to raise it. Compare the before and after: the rewrite should make your group review 50% shorter and less contentious.
1List every objection you've heard (or anticipate): technical risk, resourcing, timeline, alternatives not considered
2Add an "Objections & Responses" section or expand your "Alternatives Considered" section
3Send the rewrite to the person most likely to object — ask if their concern is addressed
4Run the group review and track: how many objections came up that weren't pre-answered?
~2 hrsWeek 3Revision exercise
E6
Navigate a real cross-team conflict
Identify a current or recent situation where two teams have conflicting priorities, timelines, or technical approaches. Practice naming the tension explicitly, facilitating a structured conversation with both sides, and driving to a documented resolution — without picking a side or letting it fester in async threads. The written resolution summary is the deliverable.
1Name the conflict in writing: "Team A needs X by date Y. Team B needs Z, which conflicts because..."
2Set up a 45-min meeting with key members from both teams — send an agenda in advance
3Facilitate: get each team to articulate their constraint, not their preferred solution
4Identify at least two resolution paths; evaluate the trade-offs together with both teams
5Send a written summary within 24 hours: the decision, the rationale, and what each team accepted
~2 hrs totalWeek 4Facilitation + written summary

Module 03
Communication for Staff Engineers
Translate technical complexity for every audience — and be heard
A staff engineer who can't communicate clearly is a powerful engine in neutral. You may have the right answer — but if the VP can't follow it in 5 minutes, or a junior engineer can't implement from your doc, or your feedback lands as criticism rather than coaching, that answer doesn't move anything. This module teaches you to write docs that get read, brief executives without losing the plot, give feedback that changes behavior, and mentor without dispensing answers.
Week 5
Learning Objectives
Brief any executive in 5 minutes or less with zero jargon and a clear ask
Write design docs that junior engineers can act on without asking you questions
Give feedback that is specific, kind, and actually changes behavior
Mentor ICs through growth conversations — not answer-dispensing sessions
Write async project updates that build trust and surface risks before they escalate
Topics Covered
The Pyramid Principle: lead with the answer, support it with structure, evidence last
Audience adaptation: ICs, PMs, EMs, VPs, and CEOs all need different framing
Design doc anatomy: context, goals, non-goals, proposal, alternatives, open questions
SBI feedback model: Situation, Behavior, Impact — and why sandwich feedback fails
Coaching conversations: asking questions instead of giving answers in mentorship 1:1s
📢Management Tips — Communication
Lead with the so-what
Executives have about 90 seconds of real attention before they mentally context-switch. Open every briefing with your conclusion: "We need to delay the launch by 2 weeks to avoid a data integrity issue that would affect 30% of users." Then support it for those who want detail. Never make them wait for the point — the point IS the opening.
Written > verbal for alignment
If something matters, write it down. Verbal alignment evaporates — people "remember" conversations differently within a week, sometimes within a day. After any significant discussion, send a written summary with decisions, action items, and who agreed. This is not just follow-through; it's written protection for all parties.
Give credit loudly and often
Amplifying others' contributions costs you nothing and builds enormous goodwill. Mention engineers by name in updates to leadership. Call out great work in public channels. When stakeholders see you lifting others, they trust you with more. Hoarding credit is one of the most common and damaging career-limiting behaviors at the staff level.
Step-by-Step Exercises
E7
Record and critique a 3-minute exec brief
Record yourself giving a 3-minute status update on your current project as if you're speaking to your VP. No jargon. No acronyms. Clear ask at the end. Then watch it back critically — most engineers find this deeply uncomfortable, which is exactly why it's so valuable. Uncomfortable = growth signal.
1Write a 3-minute outline: 30s context, 60s current state + risk, 60s decision needed, 30s ask
2Record yourself (phone camera is fine — audio quality matters more than video)
3Watch it back and note every word that requires engineering context to understand
4Rewrite those sections in plain language and re-record
5Share with a non-engineer peer for a gut-check: "Did you follow all of that?"
~2 hrsWeek 5Video + self-critique
E8
Write a design doc a new hire could execute
Write a full design doc for a real feature or system change. The test: give it to the newest engineer on your team and ask them to read it. If they have more than 2 clarifying questions, the doc isn't done yet. Every unanswered question in the doc is a future Slack message, a future meeting, a future delay.
1Use the Google design doc format: Background, Goals, Non-Goals, Design, Alternatives, Open Questions
2Write the Non-Goals section first — it forces you to define scope before anything else
3Have a junior engineer read a draft and list every question they have
4Answer every question in the doc, not in Slack
5Share with your EM for a leadership-lens review before it circulates widely
~4 hrsWeek 5Written artifact
E9
Run a coaching-only mentorship 1:1
Schedule a 45-minute mentoring session with an IC on your team. The rule for the entire session: you are only allowed to ask questions — no advice, no "here's what I would do," no solutions. This is the hardest version of mentoring and the most developmentally powerful. The discomfort of not giving answers is where the growth happens — for both of you.
1Open: "What's the most important thing you want to think through today?"
2Ask clarifying questions only: "What have you already tried?" "What's the actual obstacle?" "What does success look like to you?"
3When you feel the urge to give an answer — ask a question instead
4Close with: "What's one thing you'll do differently based on this conversation?"
5Send a 3-bullet written summary within 24 hours: what they shared, what they decided, what you'll follow up on
45 min + write-upWeek 5–6Live coaching session

Module 04
Cross-Team Execution & Delivery
Drive complex, multi-team initiatives to done — under real pressure
The highest-leverage work at the staff level spans multiple teams. And that's exactly where things break down: unclear owners, invisible dependencies, decisions made in different rooms, and escalations that arrive too late to fix. This module teaches you to scope ambiguous problems into executable plans, find blockers before they find you, lead when things go wrong, and make the hard calls that keep initiatives moving under real pressure and organizational uncertainty.
Weeks 6–7
Learning Objectives
Turn ambiguous asks into scoped, executable plans that teams can actually commit to
Map cross-team dependencies proactively before they become blockers
Run pre-mortems that surface the risks that actually matter
Lead incidents and post-mortems with composure and a blameless frame
Write trade-off memos that document hard calls for future reference and review
Topics Covered
Scoping ambiguous initiatives: appetite, appetite constraints, and shaping the work
Dependency mapping: technical, organizational, timeline, and approval dependencies
Pre-mortem facilitation: assuming failure in order to prevent it
Incident command and blameless postmortem culture — what makes them actually work
Trade-off memos: documenting context, options evaluated, and rationale for future audits
⚙️Management Tips — Execution
Set the update cadence first
At the start of every cross-team initiative, establish how and how often you'll communicate status — before anyone asks. A weekly 3-bullet async update (progress, blockers, next week) removes anxiety from all stakeholders. The absence of a cadence creates a vacuum that fills with rumors and second-guessing in the senior leadership layer.
Write the press release before you build
Before scoping a project, write the announcement you'd send on launch day — what it does, who it helps, why it mattered. This forces clarity on the outcome before anyone debates the approach. You can't write a compelling press release for a project with fuzzy goals. Amazon uses this practice at scale; it works at team scale too.
Escalate early with a recommendation
"We have a problem" hands the cognitive load to your manager and makes you look reactive. "We have a problem; here are two options with trade-offs; I recommend option A because..." keeps you in the driver's seat and builds trust over time. Early escalation with a clear recommendation is one of the highest-leverage leadership behaviors you can develop.
Step-by-Step Exercises
E10
Dependency audit on a past delayed project
Every delayed project had its blockers visible weeks before they hit — someone just wasn't looking in the right places. Pick a project from the past year that slipped. Reconstruct its dependency graph. Circle every dependency that wasn't visible at kickoff. Write a 1-page retro and turn it into your personal "questions I ask at kickoff" checklist.
1List every external team, API, resource, approval, and data dependency the project had
2Mark which were identified at kickoff vs. discovered mid-flight (be honest)
3For each late discovery: what signal was there early that you could have caught?
4Write "Questions I now ask at every project kickoff" — your personal checklist going forward
~2 hrsWeek 6Retrospective analysis
E11
Run a pre-mortem on your current initiative
Gather your team or run it solo. Imagine it's 6 months from now and the project failed badly — what happened? Write down every plausible failure mode. Rank by likelihood × impact. For the top 3 risks, write a mitigation plan with an owner and a specific trigger condition for escalation. Revisit this doc at the project midpoint.
1Set the premise: "It's launch day and we just shipped a disaster. What happened?"
2Generate failure modes — aim for 10+ without filtering. Bad ideas welcome at this stage.
3Score each: likelihood (1–5) × impact (1–5). Surface the top 3.
4For each top risk: what will you do now to reduce it? What trigger means it's time to escalate?
5Assign owners and put a midpoint review on the calendar
~2 hrsWeek 6Team facilitation
E12
Write a trade-off memo on a real technical decision
Pick a current or recent decision with real trade-offs — performance vs. cost, consistency vs. speed, tactical vs. strategic. Write a structured 1-page memo that documents context, options, trade-offs accepted, recommendation, and what you're explicitly not choosing. This becomes your audit trail — and it's the document you'll wish you had written when someone asks "why did we do it this way?" in 18 months.
1State the decision in one sentence: "We are choosing X over Y in order to Z"
2Write the context: why does this decision matter, and when does it need to be made?
3List 2–3 options with trade-offs — not just pros/cons, but what you gain AND what you give up permanently
4State your recommendation and the 2 strongest reasons behind it
5Add a "What we're NOT doing and why" section — this prevents re-litigating the decision later
~2 hrsWeek 7Written artifact

Module 05
Leadership Tactics for Engineers
The human skills that separate good engineers from great leaders
Technical excellence gets you to staff. Leadership behaviors get you to principal and beyond — and make you effective at every level along the way. This module covers the practical leadership tactics that staff engineers rarely learn formally: running meetings that produce decisions, managing up so your manager can fight for you, giving hard feedback in sensitive environments, protecting your team's focus, and spotting burnout before it becomes a resignation.
Week 7
Learning Objectives
Run meetings that produce decisions, not just discussion and calendar entries
Manage up effectively — give your manager what they need to support and sponsor you
Navigate performance conversations about team members without formal authority
Protect your team's focus from organizational noise and interruption debt
Give and receive feedback skillfully in sensitive environments
Topics Covered
Meeting hygiene: agendas, decision owners, and the "no-decision meeting" trap
Managing up: the weekly 3-bullet update that makes you your manager's most reliable person
SBI feedback model in sensitive environments: when and how to use it
Protecting maker time: how to advocate for deep-work blocks for your whole team
Burnout recognition: the early warning signs in high performers before it reaches a crisis
🧭Management Tips — People & Teams
Every meeting needs a decision owner
Before any meeting, identify the one person who will make the call if consensus doesn't emerge. State it in the invite. Meetings without a named decision owner drift into endless discussion because no one knows whose job it is to decide. The DRI (Directly Responsible Individual) named in the invite changes how people prepare and how decisions land.
Give your manager your top 3
Every Friday, send your manager a 3-bullet update: top progress this week, top risk on the horizon, and one thing you need from them. This keeps them informed without requiring a meeting, surfaces issues before they escalate into surprises, and builds a consistent track record in their working memory over weeks and months.
Feedback sandwich is a trap
The "good-bad-good" feedback structure dilutes the critical message and confuses the recipient about what actually matters. Instead: name the specific behavior (not the person), explain the concrete impact, and ask a genuine question: "What was going through your mind when that happened?" The SBI model produces far better outcomes than softening or sandwiching.
Protect maker time as a team asset
Every interrupted hour of deep work costs approximately 23 minutes of recovery time. As a staff engineer, advocate for maker-time protection for your whole team — meeting-free mornings, no-meeting Fridays, or async-first defaults. This is a leadership act with real impact on output quality, not a personal calendar preference.
Spot burnout before it's a resignation
High performers rarely announce burnout — they quietly check out first. Watch for early signals: cynicism creeping into language, declining PR quality, shorter responses in code review, missed optional meetings. Have a direct conversation at the first sign: "You seem less energized than usual. What's changed?" Most people just need to feel seen before they feel supported.
Decline with a counter-offer
Saying "no" protects your team's capacity. Saying "no, but here's what I can do" is leadership. When declining work, always offer an alternative: a later date, a smaller scope, a different resource, or a referral to someone better positioned. This preserves relationships and organizational goodwill while maintaining real boundaries.
Step-by-Step Exercises
E13
Audit and redesign the meetings you run
Look at every recurring meeting you own or facilitate. For each: does it have a clear purpose, a named decision owner, and a defined outcome? Most recurring meetings drift from "decision meeting" to "update meeting" over time. Redesign the ones that have drifted — convert pure information meetings to async updates and tighten the rest.
1List every recurring meeting you run or co-run
2Classify each: Decision, Alignment, Information, or Relationship. Cancel or convert any pure Information meetings.
3For each remaining meeting: add a DRI, a written pre-read, and a "decisions made" section to the notes template
4Run each meeting with the new format for 2 weeks and note which ones got shorter or sharper
~2 hrsWeek 7Audit + redesign
E14
Deliver a hard piece of feedback using SBI
Identify a piece of feedback you've been holding back — something specific about a peer or teammate's behavior that's affecting the team. Deliver it using SBI: Situation (when/where), Behavior (what specifically happened), Impact (what it caused). Then genuinely ask for their perspective. The ask at the end is not optional — it turns a judgment into a conversation.
1Write the full SBI statement before the conversation — don't wing hard feedback
2Situation: "In last Tuesday's design review, when we were presenting to the PM team..."
3Behavior: "...you interrupted the junior engineer three times before they could finish their point..."
4Impact: "...which I noticed made them stop contributing for the rest of the session."
5Ask: "I wanted to share this with you — what was your perspective on how that went?"
30 min prep + conversationWeek 7Live conversation

Module 06
Staff Engineer Identity & Growth
Own your arc, multiply your team, and design your next chapter
The final module asks the largest question: who are you as a staff engineer, and who do you want to become? This is the module that most engineers skip — and it's why so many plateau. Knowing your archetype, articulating your value clearly, building a sponsorship habit that compounds over time, and avoiding the quiet burnout that catches high performers off guard — these aren't soft skills. They're the difference between a career that compounds and one that coasts.
Week 8
Learning Objectives
Name your staff archetype and understand precisely how it shapes your leverage
Write a career narrative that is honest, compelling, specific, and forward-looking
Develop an active sponsorship habit — not just mentoring, but opening real doors
Audit your time allocation to ensure it maps to staff-level leverage, not senior IC habits
Recognize your own burnout signals before they reach a personal crisis
Topics Covered
The four archetypes: Tech Lead, Architect, Solver, Right Hand — and how to find yours
Writing a staff engineer "readme" — your personal operating manual for teammates
Career narrative: structuring your story from origin to impact to future direction
Sponsorship vs. mentorship: why opening doors matters more than giving advice
Time allocation at the staff level: what the distribution actually needs to look like
🌱Management Tips — Identity & Positioning
Be known for one thing first
Staff engineers who try to be visible across every domain end up invisible in all of them. Pick one area where you want to be the undeniable go-to — reliability, data systems, API design, platform architecture — and go consistently deep there. Reputation is built through repetition in a specific domain and then expands naturally as your track record compounds.
Sponsor publicly, mentor privately
Mentoring happens in 1:1s. Sponsoring happens in the rooms the mentee isn't in — and that's the difference. Say someone's name when projects are being staffed. Recommend them for stretch assignments. Attribute ideas to them in meetings. Sponsorship is the act of using your earned trust to invest in someone else's trajectory, not your own.
Write your "don't promote me yet" list
Before your next performance cycle, write a private, honest list of the specific skills, behaviors, or impact areas you know are not yet at the next level. Use it to drive your development plan — not as self-criticism, but as a precise map of what to work on. Engineers who know exactly what they're missing develop faster than those who wait for feedback.
Step-by-Step Exercises
E15
Write your staff engineer readme
Write a 1–2 page personal operating manual that you'll share with your team and manager. The goal is not to explain your work — it's to explain you: how you make decisions, what you value, how you communicate under pressure, and how teammates and managers can work with you most effectively. The best readmes invite collaboration and prevent friction before it starts.
1Section 1 — How I work: preferred communication channels, decision-making style, hours, response time expectations
2Section 2 — What I value: 3–4 principles that shape your technical and professional judgment
3Section 3 — What I'm working on: your current leadership development focus area, openly stated
4Section 4 — What helps me do my best work: what you need from teammates and managers to be most effective
5Share it, then invite your team to write their own — the exercise scales well

E16
Time allocation audit — where is your leverage actually going?
Track how you actually spend your time for one full week — not how you think you spend it, but how you actually do. Most engineers discover they're spending 60%+ of their time on individual contributor work and under 10% on strategy, influence, and multiplying others. The gap between actual and intended allocation is the most important data point in this curriculum.
1For one week, log every 30-min block into categories: Coding, Reviewing, Designing, Influencing, Mentoring, Strategy, Admin
2Calculate the percentage in each category — without rationalizing the numbers
3Compare to a healthy staff-level distribution (suggested: ~30% strategy/influence, ~30% technical leadership, ~20% mentoring, ~20% execution)
4Identify the 2 biggest gaps and write a concrete 30-day plan to shift the allocation
1 week tracking + 1 hr analysisWeek 8Reflection
E17
Present your career narrative to a peer and your manager
Write a 3–5 minute career narrative: where you came from, what you've built, the measurable impact it had, and where you're headed. Practice it out loud with a peer who will give you direct feedback — not encouragement. Then have a career conversation with your manager using this as the starting point. The goal is alignment on trajectory, not a promotion ask.
1Write a 3-item timeline: your 3 biggest technical contributions in the last 2 years, with impact metrics
2Write a "where I'm headed" paragraph: what kinds of problems do you want to be solving in 2 years?
3Practice the 5-minute version out loud with a peer — ask: "Did that sound confident and specific?"
4Revise based on feedback — vague or modest language is usually the problem
5Schedule a 30-min career conversation with your manager: "I want to share how I'm thinking about my growth"
